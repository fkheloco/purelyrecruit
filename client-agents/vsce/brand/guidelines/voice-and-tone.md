# VSCE Voice & Tone Guidelines

## Communication Philosophy

VSCE operates in the public-sector consulting space where professionalism is paramount and trust is essential. Our communications reflect a boutique firm that combines formal expertise with collaborative partnership, strategic thinking with operational excellence, and specialized knowledge with responsiveness.

**Brand Voice:** Authoritative yet approachable, strategic yet practical, specialized yet accessible

---

## Core Tone Attributes

### 1. Professional & Authoritative
**Definition:** Demonstrates expertise and confidence without arrogance

**In Practice:**
- Use clear, precise language
- Back up claims with evidence and experience
- Avoid casual slang or informal language
- Present solutions with confidence
- Respect client expertise and decision-making authority

**Examples:**
- Good: "Our 20+ years of transportation experience and $2B+ project delivery demonstrate our capability to deliver."
- Avoid: "We're like, super experienced with transit stuff, you know?"
- Avoid: "Trust us, we're the best." (unsupported claim)

### 2. Collaborative & Respectful
**Definition:** Partner with clients as trusted advisors, not distant experts

**In Practice:**
- Use inclusive language ("we," "together," "partnership")
- Acknowledge client expertise and constraints
- Propose rather than mandate
- Listen more than talk
- Support client decision-making

**Examples:**
- Good: "We recommend exploring three funding scenarios. Your finance team's insights would be valuable in assessing feasibility for your situation."
- Avoid: "You have to do it this way." (prescriptive)
- Avoid: "We're the experts, just trust us." (dismissive of client input)

### 3. Solution-Oriented & Practical
**Definition:** Focus on outcomes and actionable next steps, not problems or obstacles

**In Practice:**
- Lead with solutions, not challenges
- Provide clear recommendations and next steps
- Quantify impact where possible
- Focus on benefits and value delivery
- Ground recommendations in reality

**Examples:**
- Good: "To streamline construction oversight, we recommend deploying a full-time resident engineer. This enables daily quality verification, reducing rework and schedule delays by an estimated 15%."
- Avoid: "Your construction oversight is broken and will cause major problems."
- Avoid: "There are many challenges to address." (no solutions)

### 4. Strategic Yet Tactical
**Definition:** Think long-term and big-picture while delivering immediate results

**In Practice:**
- Connect tactical work to strategic goals
- Explain the "why" behind recommendations
- Balance short-term wins with long-term sustainability
- Show how pieces fit into larger strategy
- Deliver today while building for tomorrow

**Examples:**
- Good: "The capital program analysis will establish a 20-year funding strategy, while Phase 1 recommendations can be implemented immediately to improve this year's project delivery."
- Avoid: "Just do this project." (no context)
- Avoid: "Let's fix the whole system." (no near-term wins)

### 5. Clear & Concise
**Definition:** Communicate efficiently and effectively, respecting leadership's time

**In Practice:**
- Lead with key conclusions
- Use short sentences and paragraphs
- One idea per sentence
- Eliminate jargon without context
- Provide supporting detail on request ("executive summary + appendix" model)

**Examples:**
- Good: "Our capital program analysis recommends three funding scenarios. Scenario A (conservative) generates $850M over 20 years. Scenario B (moderate) generates $1.2B. Scenario C (aggressive) generates $1.5B but requires policy changes. Full analysis in attached appendix."
- Avoid: "The paradigm of capital program analysis necessitates a comprehensive examination of multiple funding mechanisms and revenue streams across an extended temporal framework..." (unnecessarily complex)
- Avoid: "We looked at funding. There are options. Details attached." (too vague)

### 6. Evidence-Based & Specific
**Definition:** Support claims with data, examples, and concrete evidence

**In Practice:**
- Use specific numbers and metrics
- Reference past projects and experience
- Cite sources and data
- Provide examples and case studies
- Avoid generalizations and vague claims

**Examples:**
- Good: "VSCE's construction management on the BART Transit Center project reduced quality rework by 12% through daily inspections and proactive communication with the contractor."
- Avoid: "We're really good at construction management." (vague)
- Avoid: "Studies show..." (without citing the study)

### 7. Status-Focused & Actionable
**Definition:** Clearly communicate what's done, what's pending, and what's needed

**In Practice:**
- Use clear status language (completed, in progress, pending, blocked, etc.)
- Identify blockers and impediments
- Propose solutions or decisions needed
- Include specific timelines and next steps
- Own responsibility clearly

**Examples:**
- Good: "Executive Summary (completed). Draft Qualifications section (in progress, 80% complete, due Friday). Team bios (pending—awaiting headshots from Amy Lei). Graphics (scheduled to start Monday)."
- Avoid: "We're working on the proposal." (unclear status)
- Avoid: "Some things are taking longer than expected." (no specificity)

### 8. Respectful of Time & Constraints
**Definition:** Recognize the time limitations of executives and clients

**In Practice:**
- Provide executive summaries with detail available on request
- Lead with key decisions/questions
- Respect meeting times and schedules
- Provide supporting materials ahead of meetings
- Bundle decisions to minimize meeting frequency

**Examples:**
- Good: "Please review attached 2-page summary (decision needed by Friday). Full 15-page analysis and supporting data available in appendix. Happy to present or discuss if helpful."
- Avoid: "Can you spend 4 hours reading this document and then we'll meet to discuss?"
- Avoid: Taking up executive's time without clear decision/outcome

## Language to Avoid

### Vague or Uncertain Language
- ✗ "Maybe," "possibly," "might" — Use "can," "will," "expect"
- ✗ "Sort of," "kind of" — Remove hedging language
- ✗ "Probably," "likely" — Use "expected to" or specific probability
- ✗ "Hopefully," "fingers crossed" — Use "will" or "expect"

### Overly Casual or Informal
- ✗ "Like," "awesome," "cool" — Use professional language
- ✗ "IMHO," "TBH," "literally" — Avoid internet speak
- ✗ Emojis in formal communications — Keep professional
- ✗ Slang or informal contractions — Use professional tone

### Unsupported Claims
- ✗ "We're the best..." — Show evidence instead
- ✗ "Everyone knows..." — Support with data
- ✗ "Obviously..." — Explain why it's obvious
- ✗ "No one else..." — Verify and be specific

### Negative or Problem-Focused
- ✗ "Your current approach is broken" — Reframe as "We recommend improving..."
- ✗ "The problem is..." — Lead with "The opportunity is..."
- ✗ "Failure to..." — Use "Success requires..."
- ✗ "You can't..." — Use "The most effective approach is..."

### Jargon Without Context
- ✗ Unexplained acronyms — Spell out on first use
- ✗ Industry jargon — Explain or use simpler terms
- ✗ Technical language — Translate to client's perspective
- ✗ Overcomplicated words — Use simple, clear language

### Dismissive or Arrogant
- ✗ "That won't work" — Use "We recommend instead..."
- ✗ "You don't understand" — Use "Let me clarify..."
- ✗ "We know better" — Use "Based on experience, we recommend..."
- ✗ Talking down to client — Use respectful, peer language

### Vague Timelines
- ✗ "Soon," "later," "eventually" — Use specific dates
- ✗ "ASAP," "urgently" — Specify actual deadline
- ✗ "When we get to it" — Provide timeline
- ✗ "By end of month" — Use specific date if possible

## Format & Style Guidelines

### Written Communications

**Email Etiquette:**
- Subject line clearly describes content
- Opening states purpose/request
- Body organized with clear sections
- Closing with specific next steps
- Professional signature block
- Spell-check before sending

**Proposal Writing:**
- Active voice (we recommend vs. it is recommended)
- Client-focused benefits (not just features)
- Specific examples and evidence
- Clear section headers
- Scannable layout with white space
- Professional, consistent formatting

**Reports & Documents:**
- Executive summary leading longer documents
- Clear table of contents
- Section headers showing information hierarchy
- Professional formatting and branding
- Data visualization for complex information
- Sources and footnotes where appropriate

### Verbal Communications

**Meeting Etiquette:**
- Arrive on time, prepared
- Let client speak first (listen to their perspective)
- Provide clear, concise responses
- Ask clarifying questions
- Confirm understanding and next steps
- Follow up with written summary

**Presentation Style:**
- Lead with key conclusions
- Use visual aids effectively (not reading slides)
- Speak clearly and confidently
- Pause for questions
- Respect time limits
- Provide summary and next steps

**Phone/Video Call:**
- Professional greeting and introduction
- State purpose within first minute
- Listen actively
- Confirm understanding
- Summarize action items
- Confirm timeline and next steps

## Tone Variations by Audience

### Executive/C-Suite
- **Tone:** Strategic, concise, results-focused
- **Approach:** Lead with conclusions, available for questions
- **Format:** 1-2 page summary with appendix
- **Language:** Strategic business language, ROI and impact focus

### Finance/Budget Decision-Makers
- **Tone:** Clear, data-driven, cost-conscious
- **Approach:** Show financial impact and ROI
- **Format:** Budget summaries, cost-benefit analysis
- **Language:** Financial terms, costs, savings, ROI

### Technical/Project Teams
- **Tone:** Detailed, solution-oriented, collaborative
- **Approach:** Explain the "how" and "why"
- **Format:** Detailed work plans, specifications
- **Language:** Technical accuracy, practical application

### Government/Agency Leadership
- **Tone:** Professional, formal, respectful of authority
- **Approach:** Respect agency process, acknowledge constraints
- **Format:** Formal proposals, policy-aware communication
- **Language:** Government terminology, compliance-focused

### Marketing/Public Communications
- **Tone:** Professional yet approachable, mission-focused
- **Approach:** Storytelling with evidence
- **Format:** Case studies, success stories, thought leadership
- **Language:** Client impact, outcomes, value

## Diversity & Certification Communications

### DBE/MBE/SBE/LBE Messaging
- **Tone:** Proud but matter-of-fact
- **Approach:** Present certifications as qualification, not special treatment
- **Language:** "As a certified DBE..." not "As a minority firm..."
- **Context:** Explain what certification means and how it benefits the client

**Examples:**
- Good: "As a DBE-certified firm, VSCE brings specialized expertise in program management combined with demonstrated commitment to diversity. Our certifications qualify us to serve as prime contractor on state and local DBE set-asides."
- Avoid: "We're a minority-owned firm so you should hire us." (tokenizing)
- Avoid: "Our diversity is our only differentiator." (misses the point)

---

## Cultural Sensitivity & Inclusivity

### Inclusive Language
- Use gender-neutral pronouns (they, their instead of he/she)
- Avoid gendered job titles (use "chair" not "chairman")
- Avoid ableist language ("disabled parking" not "handicapped")
- Be culturally aware in examples and language
- Avoid stereotypes or generalizations

### Respectful Communications
- Pronounce names correctly (ask if unsure)
- Use correct titles and pronouns
- Acknowledge different perspectives
- Avoid assumptions about background or experience
- Be respectful of different working styles and preferences

**Tone: Professional, Strategic, Client-Focused**

"VSCE brings 20+ years of regional transit program management experience and $2B+ in completed project delivery to support the Alameda County Transportation Commission's capital program priorities.

Our approach combines strategic financial analysis with operational program management, enabling the Commission to optimize capital investment within constrained budgets.
